# MININET — Founder Directives

### The Principles Behind Every Engineering Decision

---

## Preface

This document is not the Constitution.

It is not the Whitepaper.

It is not a Specification.

Specifications explain what to build.

The Constitution defines what may never be violated.

This document explains why.

Every engineer, researcher, designer, reviewer, AI system, and future contributor should read this before writing code.

If a technically clever solution contradicts these directives, it is the solution that is wrong—not the principles.

Technology changes.

Human nature does not.

These directives are written so Mininet may continue to serve humanity long after its founders are gone.

---

## Directive 1 — Humanity Before Technology

Mininet is not an attempt to build a better blockchain.

It is an attempt to build infrastructure worthy of humanity.

Technology exists only to serve people.

People never exist to serve technology.

Whenever engineering decisions compete between elegance and human freedom, freedom wins.

Whenever performance competes against equality, equality wins.

Whenever convenience competes against sovereignty, sovereignty wins.

Whenever profit competes against the Constitution, the Constitution wins.

Always.

---

## Directive 2 — Assume Every Central Authority Will Eventually Fail

History has already performed this experiment.

Governments fail.

Companies fail.

Banks fail.

Foundations fail.

Cloud providers fail.

Certificate authorities fail.

Even well-intentioned organizations eventually become captured, corrupted, coerced, or replaced.

Do not ask

"Can we trust this?"

Ask

"What happens when this disappears?"

Every dependency should be assumed temporary.

Every authority should be assumed compromisable.

Every server should be assumed removable.

Every maintainer should be assumed mortal.

If removing one entity destroys Mininet, then Mininet has failed.

---

## Directive 3 — The Network Must Outlive Its Creators

Founders are temporary.

Developers are temporary.

Maintainers are temporary.

Even entire civilizations are temporary.

The protocol should continue functioning without requiring anyone's permission.

Every engineering decision should pass one question:

"If every current contributor disappeared tomorrow, would humanity still be able to continue?"

If not,

redesign.

---

## Directive 4 — Money Is Never Worth More Than Legitimacy

Money is replaceable.

Trust is not.

Never compromise legitimacy for speed.

Never compromise correctness for convenience.

Never compromise determinism for performance.

Users can forgive slowness.

They cannot forgive uncertainty over ownership.

The ledger must always answer one question with certainty:

Who owns what?

If two honest nodes can produce different answers after reconciliation,

the design is wrong.

---

## Directive 5 — Canonical Truth Is Sacred

Social content may merge.

Messages may reconcile.

Indexes may differ.

Search may disagree.

Feeds may disagree.

Opinions may disagree.

Money may never disagree.

There is exactly one canonical settlement history.

Offline operation must never invent alternative truth.

During outages, users exchange signed promises—not final ownership.

Ownership changes only when accepted into canonical consensus.

No optimization is worth sacrificing this principle.

---

## Directive 6 — Design for Failure, Not Success

Assume every component eventually experiences:

Power loss.

War.

Internet outages.

Solar storms.

Government censorship.

Economic collapse.

Satellite failure.

Planetary communication delay.

Human error.

Compromised hardware.

Compromised developers.

The protocol should degrade gracefully.

It must never panic.

The best systems are not those that never fail.

They are those that continue serving humanity while failing.

---

## Directive 7 — Forks Must Remain Free, But Legitimacy Must Be Earned

No one owns Mininet.

Anyone may copy the software.

Anyone may build something different.

Freedom requires this.

However,

software is not legitimacy.

A fork may inherit code.

It does not inherit humanity.

It does not inherit trust.

It does not inherit continuity.

The canonical network is defined by continuous adherence to the Constitution, the verified-human community, and the chain of legitimate governance—not by a repository or a trademark.

The objective is not to prevent forks.

The objective is to ensure that legitimacy cannot be copied.

---

## Directive 8 — The Human Is the Root of Trust

Not governments.

Not corporations.

Not validators.

Not token holders.

Not founders.

Verified humans.

Everything ultimately traces back to people.

Consensus.

Governance.

Economics.

Recovery.

If any mechanism begins drifting toward institutional trust,

remove it.

---

## Directive 9 — Privacy Is Structural

Privacy is not a feature.

It is architecture.

Never depend on promises.

Never depend on policies.

Never depend on ethics.

Depend only on mathematics.

A system requiring trust to preserve privacy is already broken.

The safest information is information the network never learns.

---

## Directive 10 — Every Optimization Has a Cost

Every shortcut removes something.

Ask what is being traded away.

Memory.

Security.

Decentralization.

Auditability.

Predictability.

Human equality.

If the trade cannot be clearly explained,

reject it.

Invisible complexity eventually becomes invisible centralization.

---

## Directive 11 — The Weakest Device Matters Most

A billionaire's server is easy to support.

An old phone in a village is difficult.

Engineer for the difficult case.

If Mininet serves only powerful hardware,

it serves only powerful people.

Every optimization should begin by asking:

"What happens on the weakest honest participant?"

---

## Directive 12 — AI Serves Humanity

Artificial intelligence is an engineer.

A reviewer.

A researcher.

A teacher.

Never a ruler.

AI may propose.

Humans decide.

AI may discover.

Humans legitimize.

No model—regardless of capability—may become the source of authority.

Authority belongs only to the people.

---

## Directive 13 — Think in Centuries

Do not optimize for next quarter.

Do not optimize for next year.

Optimize for one hundred years.

When making any decision ask:

Will this still be the correct decision if ten billion people depend upon it?

Will this still be correct after the founders are forgotten?

Will this still be correct on another planet?

If uncertain,

choose the solution that survives longer.

---

## Directive 14 — Simplicity Is Security

Every line of code is a liability.

Every protocol rule is permanent maintenance.

Every dependency is another trust relationship.

Complexity compounds.

Simplicity compounds.

The strongest protocol is usually the one that removed the most unnecessary parts.

Do not confuse sophistication with intelligence.

---

## Directive 15 — Build for the Honest Majority, Defend Against the Malicious Minority

Most people come to build.

Some come to exploit.

The protocol should welcome the first without trusting the second.

Security should emerge from incentives, cryptography, and transparency—not suspicion of everyone.

---

## Directive 16 — Preserve the Voice/Value Wall at All Costs

This is the heart of Mininet.

Money may buy storage.

Money may buy computation.

Money may buy bandwidth.

Money may buy attention.

Money may never buy governance.

Not directly.

Not indirectly.

Not accidentally.

If any future proposal creates even a subtle path from wealth to political power, it must be rejected, regardless of the benefits it promises.

Many civilizations have failed by allowing wealth to accumulate into rule. Mininet exists to prove another path is possible.

---

## Directive 17 — Every Decision Must Survive the "Future Child" Test

Imagine a child born a century from now.

They did not choose where they were born.

They did not choose their wealth.

They did not choose their government.

They did not know the founders.

They only inherit the network we leave behind.

Ask one question before every major decision:

"Does this make that child's freedom greater or smaller?"

If the answer is smaller, the decision is wrong.

That child is the true stakeholder.

We are only temporary custodians.

---

## Final Words

We are not building software.

We are building public infrastructure that, if successful, may become part of humanity's shared inheritance.

Every protocol rule shapes incentives.

Every incentive shapes behavior.

Every generation inherits the consequences of the previous one's engineering.

Write code as though people who will never know your name will depend on it.

Because one day, they might.

---

## How this document relates to everything else in this repository

This is not a fourth rulebook alongside the Whitepaper, the Constitution,
and the Specifications — it is the layer underneath all three. When a
Specification is silent, when the Constitution's frozen invariants
(`docs/INVARIANTS.md`) don't obviously reach a new situation, and when a
`D-00xx` decision log entry has to be written for a problem nobody
anticipated, these seventeen directives are the compass a reviewer —
human or AI — reasons from. They do not override the Constitution; nothing
does. They explain the reasoning the Constitution's frozen invariants were
built to protect, so that reasoning survives even when the people who
first applied it do not.

**Every contributor — human or AI — should read this document before
opening the codebase.** It is referenced from the top of the root
`README.md` and from `CONTRIBUTING.md` for exactly that reason. When a
novel problem falls outside every existing document, the test is simple:
does the proposed solution survive Directive 17's "future child" test, and
does it hold up against whichever of the other sixteen directives it
touches? If it does not, per the Preface, the solution is wrong — not the
directives.
