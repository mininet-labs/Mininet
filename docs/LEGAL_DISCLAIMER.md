# Legal Disclaimer

**Status:** Constitutional legal position of the Mininet protocol, as
understood by the Founding Cohort at the time of publication (2026-07-18).

Unless a material legal or constitutional change occurs, this body of
work should be considered complete. Future revisions are intended only
to reflect significant changes in law, protocol architecture, or
judicial interpretation rather than routine legal opinion.

---

## Constitutional nature

Mininet is a global constitutional protocol.

It is not the legal system of any country, nor does it derive its
legitimacy from any government, corporation, foundation, or other
centralized institution.

Participation in Mininet is entirely voluntary.

Every participant independently decides whether, how, and to what extent
they participate, including but not limited to:

- operating nodes,
- writing software,
- contributing research,
- providing infrastructure,
- offering commercial services,
- using the protocol privately,
- forming independent organizations,
- or choosing not to participate at all.

No person is required to join Mininet, and no participant is required to
act on behalf of any other participant.

---

## Founding Cohort

The Founding Cohort exists solely to establish the initial constitutional
framework of Mininet.

It possesses no permanent ownership, sovereign authority, privileged
governance rights, or exclusive control over the protocol.

The Founding Cohort's objective is to create a system that ultimately no
longer depends upon it.

The founder remains anonymous by design. Founder anonymity is a
constitutional characteristic of the project's bootstrap, not a source
of authority. The protocol must remain capable of operating indefinitely
without identifying, contacting, or obtaining permission from the
founder.

---

## Representation

Mininet has no universal legal, political, or constitutional
representative.

A participant may voluntarily represent:

- themselves,
- an independent organization,
- a specific service they operate,
- or a published mandate granted through Mininet's constitutional
  governance.

Such representation never extends beyond the scope of that specific
mandate.

No participant may claim to represent all users, all developers, all
node operators, the anonymous founder, or the protocol as a whole
without explicit constitutional authorization.

---

## Legal responsibility

Mininet does not exempt any participant from the laws that apply to that
participant's own activities.

Each participant remains individually responsible for the legal
consequences of the activities they voluntarily undertake.

Different forms of participation may give rise to different legal
obligations.

For example, an individual running a private node, a company operating
a hosted platform, and a regulated financial service provider may each
be subject to different legal frameworks.

Those obligations remain personal to the participant performing the
activity and do not transfer ownership or control over the protocol.

---

## Constitutional independence

National laws may regulate particular people, organizations, services,
or activities.

They do not, by themselves, amend the constitutional rules of the
Mininet protocol.

Constitutional changes occur only through Mininet's constitutional
governance process.

If compliance with a particular service model would require violating a
frozen constitutional invariant, Mininet's position is that the service
should be redesigned, limited, or not offered, rather than altering the
protocol's constitutional foundations.

---

## No ownership

No government, corporation, foundation, repository host, domain owner,
maintainer, contributor, investor, service provider, or member of the
Founding Cohort owns Mininet.

Ownership of independent software implementations, trademarks, domains,
infrastructure, or businesses does not constitute ownership of the
protocol itself.

---

## Good-faith legal position

This legal corpus reflects the Founding Cohort's good-faith
interpretation of the interaction between Mininet's constitutional
principles and applicable legal frameworks.

It is intended to guide protocol architecture and governance.

It is not legal advice to any individual participant and should not be
interpreted as a claim that any participant is exempt from applicable
law. Participants operating regulated services should obtain
jurisdiction-specific legal advice where appropriate.

---

## Maintenance status

The constitutional legal framework is considered complete.

Future revisions are expected only when one or more of the following
occur:

- material changes to applicable law;
- significant judicial decisions affecting the protocol;
- constitutional amendments adopted through governance;
- protocol changes that materially alter legal analysis.

Until such an event occurs, the legal corpus should be treated as a
stable constitutional reference rather than an actively evolving design
document.
