# Working Groups and Maintainers

**Status:** Normative scaling model

## Purpose

Working groups allow hundreds of contributors to collaborate without creating one all-powerful maintainer class.

## Suggested groups

- Constitution and Governance
- Identity and Human Verification
- Consensus and Execution
- Networking and Discovery
- Forge, Build, and Provenance
- Update, Installer, and Release
- Storage, Replication, and Erasure
- Value, Settlement, Treasury, and Bounties
- Applications and User Experience
- Security and External Audit Coordination

Groups may change as the architecture evolves.

## Roles

### Contributor

Submits work. May be anonymous, pseudonymous, or public.

### Reviewer

Authorized to review a defined domain. Persistent identity is required for review continuity; public identity is not.

### Maintainer

Coordinates domain state, reviewer assignment, and integration. Maintainer status grants responsibility, not governance weight or ownership.

### Integration Representative

Represents the group's boundary assumptions in cross-domain integration.

### Security Steward

Coordinates private vulnerability handling and external audit work under strict access control.

## Selection

Selection should be based on demonstrated evidence, reliability, constitutional understanding, and peer confidence—not payment, employer, balance, fame, or raw commit count.

## Terms and rotation

Roles should have review periods, inactivity rules, conflict disclosures, and removal procedures. Rotation reduces capture while retaining expertise.

## Decision scope

Working groups may decide ordinary implementation matters within accepted specifications. They may not unilaterally amend frozen invariants, monetary semantics, constitutional rules, or owner-adoption rights.

## Appeals

A contributor may appeal a procedural rejection to the Integration Council or governance. Appeals should examine process and evidence, not demand acceptance because of identity or status.

## Conflicts of interest

Reviewers disclose financial, employment, authorship, or close-control relationships that could affect independence. Disclosure may be privacy-preserving where possible; governance may reassign review without requiring public personal details.

## Identity and authority rule

Maintainers may be public or pseudonymous. Persistent cryptographic continuity and disclosed conflicts may be required for authority; public legal identity may not be required by default. Current identity-root uniqueness must not be described as verified personhood.
